  heading: It starts with your ordinary day
  content: 'You log the things you''re already managing: drinks against your fluid target, meals, medications as you take them, symptoms as they come and go, sleep and mood in a quick evening check-in, plus appointments and dialysis sessions.


    None of it is homework. Most entries take seconds, reminders carry the remembering, and missing a day is fine: KidneyMate picks up where you left off, without commentary.


    Alongside your entries sits your personal context: your CKD stage and the targets, limits and notes your care team sets. That context is what makes the next layer meaningful. A litre and a half of water means nothing in general. Against your target, on your treatment, it means something.'

- type: split_content
  background: gradient
  heading: Clear rules, your baseline, honest flags
  content: 'KidneyMate''s analysis does three simple, powerful things.


    It checks your records against your personal rules: the thresholds your care team sets. It compares this week with your own usual pattern, because in a long-term condition, change from your baseline matters more than any single number. And it notices unusual combinations: sleep, mood, missed doses and skipped entries drifting together in a way that isn''t like you.


    Here''s a real example of the kind of rule at work: if medications are missed three or more times in a week, that pattern is noted, weighted by your clinical context, and may become a gentle check-in for you and a review flag for your care team.


    What this layer never does is diagnose, predict doom, or decide anything. It organises and it notices. That''s the job.'

  heading: Then humans do the human part
  content: 'Flags, trends and summaries arrive on a clinician dashboard with the context needed to judge them: your recent records, your baseline, your history of what turned out to matter before.


    Your clinician reviews, and decides. Sometimes that''s "worth a call". Sometimes it''s an adjusted fluid target or an updated rule. Very often it''s "noted, all fine", and that''s a feature, not a failure: it means someone accountable looked.


    Every review feeds back into your personal knowledge base, so the analysis keeps getting more yours: your targets current, your rules tuned, past clinical judgement informing future flags. And you stay in control throughout: sharing is your choice, visible and reversible.'
