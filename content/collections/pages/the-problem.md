- type: rich_prose
  heading: It builds quietly
  background: solid
  content: 'Chronic kidney disease develops gradually, often over years, and it''s easy to miss. Kidney function declines with few early symptoms; many people feel nothing until the condition is advanced. In Australia, 2.7 million people aged 18 and over had biomedical signs of chronic kidney disease in 2022 to 2024, 14.2% of adults. Few of them know it: only 7.4% of adults with those biomedical markers self-reported having the condition. Worldwide, 788 million adults were living with CKD in 2023, more than double the number in 1990.


    By the time CKD is recognised, it asks a great deal: monitoring kidney function and blood pressure, adjusting medications, changing what you eat and drink, and watching for complications. It''s progressive, it''s linked with serious cardiovascular risk, and its global burden has risen sharply for three decades. The clinical stakes are real. But the clinical stakes are only half the story.'

- type: rich_prose
  heading: The work nobody sees
  background: gradient
  content: '> Researchers who study it describe CKD self-management as time-consuming, invasive and exhausting, for patients and for the people who care for them.


    And it''s unpaid, untrained, solo work. The tools people cobble together are notebooks, fridge magnets, phone alarms, a food app here, a reminder app there, and memory. Each covers a fragment. Nothing holds the whole picture, so nobody sees the whole picture, including the person living it. On good weeks the system holds. On hard weeks, when symptoms flare or mood drops, the tracking is the first thing to go, exactly when it matters most.'
- type: split_content
  background: solid
  heading: Meanwhile, the care team is flying on snapshots
  content: 'Clinicians managing CKD are skilled, committed and structurally blind between visits. A consultation sees you for fifteen minutes every few months. Blood tests capture single points in time. Everything else, the missed doses, the swelling that came and went, the fortnight of bad sleep, the fluid creep, arrives as recollection, if it arrives at all.


    The patterns that matter most in a long-term condition are exactly the ones that form slowly between appointments. And they''re the ones nobody is positioned to see.'
