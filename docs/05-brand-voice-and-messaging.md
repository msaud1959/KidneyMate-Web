# 05. Brand Voice and Messaging Guide

## The voice in one sentence

KidneyMate sounds like a steady, knowledgeable mate: someone who understands kidney disease deeply, talks like a person, never panics, and never overpromises.

The name is the brief. In Australia a mate is the friend who shows up, keeps their word and doesn't make a fuss. The product's voice earns the name.

## Voice principles

1. **Talk like a person who gets it.** Write from inside the experience: the fluid limit on a hot day, the tablet you cannot remember taking, the tiredness with no explanation. Specific beats sympathetic. "We know this is hard" is filler; naming the actual hard thing is empathy.

2. **Plain words, precise facts.** Say "your kidneys filter less than they should" before reaching for "estimated glomerular filtration rate". When a clinical term is needed, use it correctly and explain it once. Patient-facing pages target a year 8 reading level. Clinician pages may use full clinical vocabulary.

3. **Calm, always.** No urgency tricks, no fear, no exclamation marks in body copy. The product's own insight voice is explicitly non-alarming, and the website matches it. We can describe serious realities (rising mortality, missed patterns) with gravity instead of alarm.

4. **Honest about stage, proud of the pathway.** "In development", "designed to", "our prototype", "subject to clinical validation" are not weaknesses to hide; they are the trust story. We never let marketing tense drift into claiming things the product does not yet do.

5. **The patient is the hero.** KidneyMate is the sidekick. "You manage your health; KidneyMate keeps it organised" not "KidneyMate manages your health". Never paternal, never coaching from above, never "patients should".

6. **Clinicians are partners, not gatekeepers to flatter.** Respect their time, their accountability and their scepticism. Lead with workflow reality and evidence, skip the disruption rhetoric.

## Tone by audience

| Audience | Register | Feels like |
|----------|----------|------------|
| Patients and carers | Warm, direct, second person | A good nurse who has time for you |
| Clinicians | Professional, concrete, evidence-linked | A well-prepared colleague |
| Stakeholders and researchers | Clear, strategic, measured | A credible founder who reads the literature |

The voice is one person; only the register shifts.

## Key statistics (approved set)

Use these, with sources, and no others without verification:

- 2.7 million Australian adults, about 1 in 7, show signs of kidney disease (Australian Bureau of Statistics 2022 to 2024). Most do not know it.
- 788 million adults worldwide were living with CKD in 2023, up from 378 million in 1990 (Global Burden of Disease study).
- Global CKD mortality rose more than 40% between 1990 and 2017 (Global Burden of Disease study).
- In one study of people on haemodialysis, 94% reported fatigue, 79% cramping, 76% body aches (Flythe et al. 2018).
- Reported non-adherence to medication and diet plans in CKD ranges from 17% to 74% across studies.
- A randomised study of app-supported self-management found higher self-efficacy and slower kidney function decline than usual care (Li et al. 2020). Attribute to the literature, never to KidneyMate.

## Vocabulary

**Words we use:** steady, calm, clear, daily, routine, companion, mate, in one place, between appointments, your care team, your records, your story, plain language, shows its working, review, in development, designed with, shaped by.

**Words we avoid:** empower, revolutionise, seamless, disrupt, game-changing, cutting-edge, unlock, leverage, holistic (as decoration), journey (as filler), AI-powered (as a selling point), patients (when speaking TO patients: say "you"), suffer/sufferers (say "living with"), compliance/non-compliant (say "keeping up with" or "adherence" in clinical contexts only).

**Framing rules:**
- "People living with chronic kidney disease", never "CKD sufferers" or "the chronically ill".
- KidneyMate "supports", "organises", "notices", "flags", "prepares". It never "monitors you", "diagnoses", "predicts your health", or "keeps you safe".
- Clinicians "review", "decide", "stay in charge". The system "prepares" and "surfaces".
- Emergencies: always redirect: "KidneyMate is not for emergencies. If you are unwell right now, call 000 or contact your care team."

## Style mechanics

- Australian English throughout: colour, organise, personalised, centre, recognise, program.
- No em dashes or en dashes anywhere in site copy. Use commas, colons, parentheses or full stops. Ranges use "to" (17% to 74%).
- Sentence case for headings and buttons. No Title Case Except Proper Nouns.
- Contractions welcome in patient-facing copy (you're, it's, doesn't); lighter use on clinician and research pages.
- Numbers: numerals for statistics and data (2.7 million, 94%), words for casual counts under ten in prose.
- Oxford comma: yes.
- Exclamation marks: effectively never; one may appear in a celebratory microcopy moment (waitlist confirmation) at most.

## The product's own voice (insight copy)

Website mockups quote the app, so the app voice must be consistent: plain, specific, non-alarming, always explainable, always deferring clinical judgement. Canonical example, adapted from the framework paper:

> "Your records show several missed medication entries this week. This may be worth a look: it can matter for your care plan, and your clinician may review it."

Pattern: what was noticed, in your data, stated neutrally; why it might matter, softly; who will look at it, clearly.

## Boilerplate

**One-liner:** KidneyMate is a daily companion for people living with chronic kidney disease, and a clearer window into daily life for the clinicians who care for them.

**Short (waitlist, social):** Living with chronic kidney disease is daily work: fluids, food, medications, symptoms, sleep, mood. KidneyMate brings it into one calm place and, when you choose, turns it into clear summaries your care team can actually use. In development now, built on peer-reviewed research.

**Standard (about, press):** KidneyMate is a digital health platform in development for people living with chronic kidney disease. It brings the daily work of self-management into one place: fluid and food tracking, medication reminders, symptom logging, sleep and mood check-ins, appointments and dialysis sessions. Transparent, rule-based analysis turns those records into plain-language insights for patients and structured summaries and review flags for clinicians, who remain in charge of every clinical decision. KidneyMate began as university research in regional Victoria, is grounded in a peer-reviewed framework presented at HIKM 2026, and is on a staged pathway toward pilot programs and clinical validation. It is designed privacy-first under the Australian Privacy Principles.
