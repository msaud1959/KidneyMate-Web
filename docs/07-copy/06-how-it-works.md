## 2. Layer one: your day, recorded [split_content]

**Heading:** It starts with your ordinary day

**Body:**
You log the things you're already managing: drinks against your fluid target, meals, medications as you take them, symptoms as they come and go, sleep and mood in a quick evening check-in, plus appointments and dialysis sessions.

None of it is homework. Most entries take seconds, reminders carry the remembering, and missing a day is fine: KidneyMate picks up where you left off, without commentary.

Alongside your entries sits your personal context: your CKD stage and the targets, limits and notes your care team sets. That context is what makes the next layer meaningful. A litre and a half of water means nothing in general. Against your target, on your treatment, it means something.

## 3. Layer two: sense, made transparently [split_content]

**Heading:** Clear rules, your baseline, honest flags

**Body:**
KidneyMate's analysis does three simple, powerful things.

It checks your records against your personal rules: the thresholds your care team sets. It compares this week with your own usual pattern, because in a long-term condition, change from your baseline matters more than any single number. And it notices unusual combinations: sleep, mood, missed doses and skipped entries drifting together in a way that isn't like you.

Here's a real example of the kind of rule at work: if medications are missed three or more times in a week, that pattern is noted, weighted by your clinical context, and may become a gentle check-in for you and a review flag for your care team.

What this layer never does is diagnose, predict doom, or decide anything. It organises and it notices. That's the job.

## 4. Layer three: people review [split_content]

**Heading:** Then humans do the human part

**Body:**
Flags, trends and summaries arrive on a clinician dashboard with the context needed to judge them: your recent records, your baseline, your history of what turned out to matter before.

Your clinician reviews, and decides. Sometimes that's "worth a call". Sometimes it's an adjusted fluid target or an updated rule. Very often it's "noted, all fine", and that's a feature, not a failure: it means someone accountable looked.

Every review feeds back into your personal knowledge base, so the analysis keeps getting more yours: your targets current, your rules tuned, past clinical judgement informing future flags. The framework has a name for this: keeping a human in the loop. We think of it as the reason your family can trust the app.

## 5. A day with KidneyMate [day_timeline]

**Heading:** What it feels like in practice

**Intro:** A composite day, with fictional Sam, who's on a fluid limit and eight medications.

- **7:15 am** *(tint: medications)*
  **Morning doses, done.** A quiet reminder with breakfast. Two taps and the record's accurate, which future Sam will appreciate at the nephrologist.

- **9:40 am** *(tint: fluid)*
  **Cuppa number two, logged.** The ring shows 40% of today's limit gone. It's going to be a warm one, so Sam paces the afternoon.

- **1:00 pm** *(tint: food)*
  **Lunch, with a note.** Leftover curry. Sam logs it and KidneyMate gently notes it's a higher-potassium day, worth balancing at dinner.

- **4:30 pm** *(tint: symptoms)*
  **An insight, not an alarm.** "Your afternoon tiredness has been higher than usual this week." One tap shows why it appeared. Sam adds it to the list for Thursday's appointment.

- **8:50 pm** *(tint: sleep_mood)*
  **Two-minute wind-down.** Mood: okay. Sleep last night: patchy. Done. Across weeks, these little check-ins are what let real patterns show.

**Footnote:** On Thursday, Sam's nephrologist opens a summary of the last three months in the first minute of the appointment. The conversation starts at "I can see the tiredness trend, let's talk about it" instead of "so, how have things been?"

## 6. The explainability promise [pull_quote + rich_prose]

**Pull quote:**
If KidneyMate can't explain an insight in plain words, KidneyMate doesn't show it.

**Body:**
Every note, nudge and flag carries its reasoning: which records, which rule, which change from your baseline. This isn't a nice-to-have. Explainability is what makes the difference between a tool your care team can stand behind and an oracle nobody should trust. It's also why we built on transparent rules before reaching for more complex AI: see our approach to that on the Solution page.

## 7. Safety boundaries [disclaimer_note, style: safety]

**Heading:** Where KidneyMate stops

KidneyMate supports self-management. It does not diagnose, treat, or give medical advice, and it is not an emergency service. If you feel seriously unwell, don't log it, act on it: call 000 or contact your care team. Clinical decisions belong to your clinicians, with KidneyMate or without it.
