## 2. A silent condition [rich_prose]

**Heading:** It builds quietly

Chronic kidney disease develops gradually, often over years, and it's easy to miss. Kidney function declines with few early symptoms; many people feel nothing until the condition is advanced. In Australia, 2.7 million adults show signs of kidney disease, about one in seven, and most don't know it. Worldwide, 788 million adults were living with CKD in 2023, more than double the number in 1990.

By the time CKD is recognised, it asks a great deal: monitoring kidney function and blood pressure, adjusting medications, changing what you eat and drink, and watching for complications. It's progressive, it's linked with serious cardiovascular risk, and globally its mortality rate has risen more than 40% since 1990. The clinical stakes are real. But the clinical stakes are only half the story.

## 3. The daily burden [burden_list]

**Heading:** What managing it actually means, every day

**Intro:** People living with CKD describe the same daily load, in almost the same words. It looks like this.

- **Fluid.** *(tint: fluid)*
  A daily limit that never takes a day off. Every cup of tea, every glass of water on a 38-degree day, counted against it. Too much matters. So does too little.

- **Food.** *(tint: food)*
  A renal diet is a moving puzzle of potassium, phosphorus, sodium and protein. A banana, a slice of cheese, a takeaway meal: each one a small calculation most people were never really taught to make.

- **Medications.** *(tint: medications)*
  Multiple medicines, multiple times a day, some with food, some without, some that changed at the last appointment. Across studies, 17% to 74% of people with CKD report struggling to keep up with medication and diet plans. Not because they don't care. Because it's genuinely hard.

- **Symptoms.** *(tint: symptoms)*
  Fatigue that sleep doesn't fix, itching, cramps, swelling, breathlessness, nausea. In one study of people on haemodialysis, 94% reported fatigue, 79% cramping, 76% body aches. Which of today's symptoms matter? Which are just today? It's hard to know, so many go unmentioned.

- **Sleep and mood.** *(tint: sleep_mood)*
  Poor sleep and low mood are common companions of CKD, and they feed the rest: harder to remember medications, harder to cook properly, harder to care. They're also the parts people are least likely to bring up in a consultation.

- **Appointments and treatment.** *(tint: appointments)*
  GP visits, specialist reviews, blood tests, and for many people dialysis sessions several times a week. Each with things to remember, results to interpret, and changes to carry home into the routine.

## 4. The work nobody sees [pull_quote + rich_prose]

**Pull quote:**
Researchers who study it describe CKD self-management as time-consuming, invasive and exhausting, for patients and for the people who care for them.

**Body:**
And it's unpaid, untrained, solo work. The tools people cobble together are notebooks, fridge magnets, phone alarms, a food app here, a reminder app there, and memory. Each covers a fragment. Nothing holds the whole picture, so nobody sees the whole picture, including the person living it. On good weeks the system holds. On hard weeks, when symptoms flare or mood drops, the tracking is the first thing to go, exactly when it matters most.

## 5. The visibility gap [split_content]

**Heading:** Meanwhile, the care team is flying on snapshots

**Body:**
Clinicians managing CKD are skilled, committed and structurally blind between visits. A consultation sees you for fifteen minutes every few months. Blood tests capture single points in time. Everything else, the missed doses, the swelling that came and went, the fortnight of bad sleep, the fluid creep, arrives as recollection, if it arrives at all.

The patterns that matter most in a long-term condition are exactly the ones that form slowly between appointments. And they're the ones nobody is positioned to see.

**Stat band (style: deep):**

- **+40%**
  Rise in global CKD mortality since 1990.

**Body:**
The visibility gap isn't an inconvenience. It's where early signals get missed: the adherence slide that could have prompted a call, the symptom trend that could have brought a review forward, the deterioration that could have been slowed. For people, that can mean avoidable decline toward dialysis or transplant. For a health system already stretched, it means the most resource-intensive end of kidney care keeps growing while the cheap, early interventions keep arriving late.

## 7. Why now [icon_points]

**Heading:** Why this problem is solvable now

- **The burden is rising.** More people are living longer with CKD, and health services can't simply scale appointments to match.
- **The evidence points somewhere.** Studies of supported digital self-management in CKD have shown improved self-efficacy and encouraging clinical signals. Structured support helps; fragmented apps don't.
- **Patient-generated health data has matured.** Electronic symptom monitoring with clinician feedback has been shown to be feasible and welcomed, when it's designed around clinical review rather than around the app.
- **The missing piece is integration.** What's absent isn't another tracker. It's a clinically responsible pathway from daily records to accountable human review. That's the piece we set out to build.
