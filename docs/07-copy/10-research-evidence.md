## 2. Our framework paper [paper_feature]

**Paper title:** A Conceptual Framework for Chronic Kidney Disease Self-Management Support

**Venue:** 19th Health Informatics Knowledge Management Conference (HIKM 2026), Australia

**Plain-language abstract:**
Living with chronic kidney disease means continuous monitoring, lifestyle management, medication adherence and communication with clinicians, almost all of it happening outside the clinic. Existing digital tools address fragments: education here, reminders there, diet logging somewhere else. This paper proposes a framework that connects the fragments into a single, clinically responsible pathway with three layers. A data layer brings together patient-generated health data and a personalised, guideline-informed knowledge base. A rule-based analysis layer turns longitudinal records into transparent trends, patterns and review signals. A clinician review layer ensures human professionals interpret, validate and contextualise every output before feedback or action, with their decisions feeding back to personalise future analysis. The framework positions CKD self-management support as decision support, never autonomous clinical decision-making, and sets out the validation research needed next.

## 3. What the wider evidence says [evidence_list]

**Heading:** The shoulders this stands on

**Intro:** A working library of the research that shaped KidneyMate. Each entry says what the study found and what we took from it. This library grows as the field does.

- **The global burden is large and rising.**
  Global Burden of Disease study, CKD Collaborators (The Lancet, 2025). 788 million adults living with CKD in 2023, up from 378 million in 1990.
  *What it means for KidneyMate: this is not a niche problem, and self-management support has to scale in ways clinic hours cannot.*

- **Guidelines put daily management at the centre.**
  KDIGO 2024 Clinical Practice Guideline for CKD (Kidney International, 2024). Monitoring of kidney function, blood pressure, medication use, symptoms, diet, fluid and modifiable risk factors is core to CKD management.
  *What it means: the domains KidneyMate tracks aren't our invention; they're the guideline's own checklist, made liveable.*

- **Symptom burden is heavy and underreported.**
  Flythe et al. (CJASN, 2018). Among surveyed haemodialysis patients: fatigue 94%, cramping 79%, body aches 76%.
  *What it means: symptom tracking must be near-effortless, and trends matter more than single reports.*

- **Self-management is exhausting work.**
  Roberti et al. (BMJ Open, 2018), systematic review. CKD self-management described as time-consuming, invasive and exhausting for patients and caregivers.
  *What it means: KidneyMate must reduce burden, not add a reporting job on top.*

- **Supported digital self-management shows promise.**
  Li et al. (JMIR, 2020), randomised controlled study. App-plus-support intervention: higher self-efficacy and self-management scores, and slower eGFR decline than controls, in a small, short study.
  *What it means: encouraging signal, honest caveats; exactly why proper validation is on our roadmap.*

- **But the evidence base wants integration.**
  Stevenson et al. (Cochrane Review, 2019). eHealth interventions for CKD: evidence low-quality and heterogeneous; single-function tools dominate.
  *What it means: the gap isn't another app; it's integration and clinical accountability.*

- **Electronic symptom monitoring is feasible and welcomed.**
  Evans et al. (CJASN, 2020); Viecelli et al. (AJKD, 2022). Routine electronic symptom screening achieved high completion; patients, nurses and nephrologists found monitoring with clinician feedback feasible, given engagement and timely response.
  *What it means: the review layer isn't a nice idea; it's the condition under which monitoring works at all.*

- **Decision support fails without accountable follow-up.**
  Gulla et al. (IJMI, 2017); Litvin et al. (JABFM, 2016). CKD decision-support tools need actionable information, guideline links and trend visualisation, and alerts without clear ownership of review deliver limited value.
  *What it means: KidneyMate's design starts where these systems stopped: at who reviews, decides and follows up.*

## 4. Our validation plan [numbered_steps]

**Heading:** What we must prove, and how

1. **Expert review.** Clinicians and health informatics researchers scrutinise the framework and prototype against practice reality.
2. **Prototype refinement.** What the experts break, we rebuild. Documented, versioned, honest.
3. **Pilot implementation.** Small, governed pilots with Australian clinics and dialysis services, with proper ethics and consent.
4. **Evaluation against stated criteria.** Usability, interpretability, clinical relevance, transparency, workflow integration and stakeholder acceptance: the six tests our own paper sets for us.
5. **Publication.** Results go into the literature, whichever way they land.

## 5. What we heard in early validation [icon_points]

**Heading:** Around 50 conversations in, three themes keep repeating

- **Patients want simpler daily management.** Not more data, less juggling. One place, low effort, no guilt.
- **Clinicians want clearer, regular data.** Not raw feeds, organised signal: trends, context, and a reason attached to every flag.
- **Clinics want trust and fit.** Privacy, reliability and workflow integration decide adoption, long before feature lists do.
